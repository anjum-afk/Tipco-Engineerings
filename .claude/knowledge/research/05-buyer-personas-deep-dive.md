# Buyer Personas — Deep Dive

**Compiled:** 30 June 2026. Research-grounded analysis of how industrial process-equipment buyers in Tipco's industries actually decide.
Companion to the persona summary in [02-buyer-personas.md](02-buyer-personas.md). Market context: [03-industry-analysis.md](03-industry-analysis.md).

> This file goes beyond "who they are" into **how the decision is actually made** — the buying committee, the scoring criteria, the journey, the regulated-pharma process, discovery channels, and the fears that win or lose the deal. Each section ends with the **site/sales implication** for Tipco.

## 1. The Decision-Making Unit (DMU) — it's rarely one person

92% of B2B purchases involve 2+ people; large-organisation committees average **10–11 members**. For capital equipment the committee scales with customer size — and **collapses in SMEs**:

| Role | What they care about | SME paint unit | Large plant / pharma |
|------|---------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Economic buyer** (signs the cheque) | Price, ROI, payment terms, financing | **Owner** (also champion) | CFO / Director |
| **Champion** (drives it internally) | Solving the problem, looking good | Owner | Project Engineer / Plant Head |
| **Technical evaluator** | Specs, throughput, fit, integration | Plant manager (or owner) | Production / Project Engineer |
| **End user** (operates it) | Ease of use, cleaning, reliability | Floor supervisor | Shift / production operators |
| **Maintenance** ⚠ high influence | **Spare-parts availability**, serviceability | Owner / fitter | Maintenance head |
| **Procurement / commercial** | Quotes, terms, vendor reliability | (Owner) | Purchase dept |
| **Quality / EHS** ⚠ can block | Certs, compliance, MOC, safety | (rarely) | QA/QC, EHS — **mandatory in pharma** |
| **Executive sponsor** | Strategic fit, capex | Owner | MD / Board (for big lines) |

**Key insight:** in a **₹5–100 Cr SME paint/ink unit, the owner plays 4–5 of these roles at once** (Persona 1) — so the sale is fast, personal, trust-driven. In a **large new-entrant plant or pharma company**, it's a true multi-month committee with a technical gatekeeper (Persona 3) and a quality blocker.

→ **Implication:** the site must serve *both* the solo owner (fast specs + WhatsApp) **and** a committee (downloadable docs the champion can forward to finance, QA, and maintenance).

## 2. How the decision is scored (criteria & weighting)

Typical industrial vendor-evaluation rubric:

| Criterion | ~Weight | What it means for Tipco |
|-----------|:------:|-------------------------|
| **Technical compliance** | 35–40% | Specs match the requirement; capacity, fineness, MOC, throughput. *Needs complete spec tables + datasheets (currently missing).* |
| **Total Cost of Ownership** | 25–30% | Not price — energy, maintenance, **spare parts, downtime**, labour over the machine's life. Often exceeds purchase price. |
| **Vendor stability & support** | 15–20% | Will they exist and support me in 10 years? *Tipco's new edge: **BSE-listed**, 35+ yrs, 2 units, ISO.* |
| **Service / responsiveness** | balance | Support hours, response time, parts logistics, warranty/SLA. |

> **TCO is the most under-leveraged argument.** Buyers fixate on price; the winning vendor reframes to lifecycle cost (low maintenance, energy, genuine spares, uptime). Tipco's About page already claims "low maintenance / readily available spare parts" — the site should turn that into an explicit **TCO story** with numbers.

→ **Implication:** lead product pages with **technical depth** (40% of the score) and add a **TCO/why-Tipco** argument (energy, maintenance, spares, uptime) to neutralize price-only comparisons.

## 3. The buying journey & timeline

Capital equipment is a **slow, considered, multi-touch** purchase:

| Stage | What happens | Length |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Trigger** | New capacity / replace old / new product line / regulatory need | — |
| **Research & shortlist** | Google + IndiaMART + referrals + trade-show memory → 3–5 vendors | days–weeks |
| **Spec & evaluate** | Compare specs/datasheets, request quotes, technical Q&A | weeks |
| **Validate** | Site visit / **demo / trial run**, reference checks, (pharma: vendor audit) | weeks |
| **Negotiate & approve** | Price, terms, delivery, committee sign-off | weeks |
| **Order → commission** | Manufacture, deliver, install, (pharma: IQ/OQ/PQ) | weeks–months |

- Standard equipment: **4–8 weeks**; complex/regulated: **10–14 weeks**; large multi-machine lines / full committee: **3–18 months**.
- **Trial runs matter:** Tipco already offers lab-machine trial runs before production purchase — a strong de-risker; surface it prominently.

→ **Implication:** the site isn't a one-visit close. It must support **multiple visits** (downloadable docs to revisit), **reference/credibility content**, and a **low-friction first contact** (WhatsApp) that starts a relationship, not a form-wall.

## 4. Jobs-to-be-Done (what they're really hiring the machine — and Tipco — to do)

| Job type | The buyer's real goal |
|----------|----------------------|
| **Functional** | Grind/disperse to target fineness at target throughput, reliably, for years |
| **Emotional** | *Not get blamed* for a costly mistake; sleep easy that it won't break mid-production |
| **Social** | Look competent to the boss/board; be seen to have chosen a credible, "proper" supplier |

> The emotional + social jobs are why **credibility signals** (listed company, ISO, named clients, case studies) punch above their weight — they let the buyer **justify the choice to others and de-risk their own reputation.** This is the "nobody got fired for buying the credible vendor" dynamic.

→ **Implication:** every credibility asset Tipco currently hides (certs, clients, financials, public-company status) is directly serving the buyer's emotional/social jobs. Surfacing them isn't vanity — it's conversion.

## 5. The regulated (pharma/cosmetics) buyer — a different process entirely

Pharma buyers **cannot** buy like a paint SME. They follow a formal qualification lifecycle:

1. **URS (User Requirement Specification)** — written first; everything is judged against it.
2. **Vendor audit & qualification** — the supplier itself is audited and approved against the URS.
3. **DQ (Design Qualification)** — *before* procurement: reviews specs, drawings, **MOC, cleanability/CIP, automation, integration**.
4. **IQ → OQ → PQ** — after delivery: installation, operational range, performance vs URS. Needs **calibration certificates, MOC certificates (e.g. SS 316L), software versions, documentation**.
5. Compliance: GMP / 21 CFR 211.63.

**What this means:** a pharma buyer will **disqualify** a vendor who can't supply MOC certs, cleanability design, documentation, and validation support — regardless of price. Conversely, a vendor who speaks this language wins premium, sticky business.

→ **Implication:** if Tipco wants more pharma revenue, its pharma/lab product pages and an **"industries/pharma"** page must speak URS/qualification language and offer **documentation/MOC/CIP** content. Today the site has **no MOC or weight specs anywhere** — a hard blocker for this segment.

## 6. Discovery & information sources (where to be found)

| Channel | Role in Tipco's market |
|---------|------------------------|
| **Google search** | "bead mill for paint", "dyno mill price", "disperser manufacturer India" — intent-rich; SEO is broken today (see legacy audit). |
| **IndiaMART / TradeIndia** | ~60% B2B marketplace share; buyers filter by response rate, ratings, transaction history. Tipco has a legacy IndiaMART presence (Tipco Engineering Works). |
| **Trade shows / expos** | Paint India, IPCA, China Coating Show — Tipco actively exhibits (2022–2025). Face-to-face shortlisting. |
| **Referrals / word of mouth** | Dominant in relationship-driven SME segment. Case studies/testimonials feed this. |
| **The company website** | The credibility-check after discovery — where deals are validated or lost. |

→ **Implication:** the website's job is mostly **mid/late-funnel** (validate credibility, supply specs/docs after the buyer found Tipco elsewhere). Fast load, specs, and trust signals matter more than top-of-funnel flash. Get the blog into the sitemap and fix SEO to capture Google intent.

## 7. Fears, objections & risk — and how the site neutralizes each

The dominant emotion in capital-equipment buying is **fear of a costly mistake.** Map each fear to an on-site answer:

| Fear / objection | What neutralizes it |
|------------------|---------------------|
| **"It'll break and stop my production"** (downtime = #1 fear) | MTBF/reliability claims, robust-build proof, uptime case studies, service-response promise |
| **"I'll be held hostage on spare parts"** | "Readily available genuine spares" (Tipco already claims this) — make it explicit, with stock/lead-time |
| **"Will this vendor still exist / support me?"** | **Listed company, 35+ yrs, 2 units, financials** — Tipco's strongest new card |
| **"Will it actually hit my fineness/throughput?"** | Full spec tables, **trial run on lab machine**, demo, references |
| **"Is it worth the price?"** | TCO reframe (energy/maintenance/uptime), ROI |
| **"Can I trust an Indian SME vs an import?"** | Certs, named clients, exports (China shows), case studies; "global quality, Indian pricing" |
| **"Will I look bad if this goes wrong?"** (career risk) | Every credibility signal above = cover for the buyer |

> Sellers **lose** capital-equipment deals mainly from weak consensus-building, a thin ROI case, and slow response — not from product. The site should make it *easy for the champion to build internal consensus* (forwardable docs) and *easy to start a fast conversation*.

## Top 5 takeaways for Tipco

1. **Two buying modes:** the solo owner (fast, trust-driven) and the multi-month committee (technical + quality + finance). Serve both — fast contact *and* forwardable documentation.
2. **Technical compliance (40%) + TCO (30%) decide it** — yet Tipco's specs are thin and there's no TCO argument. Biggest fixable gap.
3. **Credibility serves the buyer's emotional/social job** (don't get blamed; look competent). Tipco's hidden assets — **listed company, ISO, clients, case studies** — are conversion tools, not vanity.
4. **Pharma is a documentation game** (URS/DQ/IQ/OQ/PQ, MOC certs). Tipco can't win it without MOC/cleanability/validation content — currently absent.
5. **Fear of downtime + spare-parts hostage are the top objections.** Lead with reliability, genuine-spares availability, and service response.
